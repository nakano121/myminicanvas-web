Every parent eventually holds a drawing that's too good for the fridge.

You know the one. The dragon with the accidentally perfect smile. The family portrait where everyone's holding hands and someone is green. You can't file it in a drawer with the other two hundred — but a drawing on paper is one juice spill away from gone.

Here are 25 ways to turn that drawing into something sturdier, sorted honestly: free things you can do tonight, small-money magic, and the big once-in-a-childhood splurges.

## Free (or nearly): tonight, with what you have

**1. Frame it properly.** Not a fridge magnet — a real frame, on a real wall, next to the adult art. Watch your child's face the first time they see their work hung like it matters.

**2. Make it a bedtime story.** A drawing is a story wearing a disguise. Ask "who's this? what happens next?" and tell it back at lights-out. It will beat every book on the shelf, because they made it. (We wrote a [step-by-step guide](/blog/how-to-turn-drawing-into-bedtime-story/) — and this is exactly the magic [My Mini Canvas](/) automates on nights you're running on empty.)

**3. Record them explaining it.** Two minutes of "and that's the dog, and that's a volcano" in their four-year-old voice. The drawing you'll keep anyway; [the voice is the part that vanishes](/blog/record-your-childs-voice/).

**4. Photograph it well and back it up.** Flat light, square-on, no shadows — [the two-minute method](/blog/how-to-photograph-kids-drawings/). This is step zero for half the ideas below.

**5. Scan it as next year's birthday-party invitation.** Their own art, their own party. Unbeatable.

**6. Make wrapping paper.** Photocopy or scan-and-print the drawing tiled across A4. Grandparents will save the paper and lose the gift.

**7. Turn it into a colouring page.** Trace the outlines (or boost contrast in any photo app) and print copies — your child colouring their *own* drawing is delightfully meta, and a lovely way to revisit old favourites. We keep [free printable pages here](/printables/) if you want ready-made ones too.

**8. Start an annual "greatest hits" folder.** One clamshell box or folder per year, ten drawings max. Curation is what turns clutter into a collection — [the no-guilt system here](/blog/preserve-kids-artwork-without-clutter/).

## Small money, big magic ($5–$40)

**9. A mug.** The classic for a reason: a drawing on a mug gets seen every single morning for years. Nearly every print shop and photo site does these.

**10. A cushion or tote bag.** The drawing leaves the house and goes to the supermarket. Prepare for strangers to compliment the artist.

**11. A t-shirt (theirs).** Kids wearing their own art walk differently. Sizing up means it lasts two summers.

**12. A calendar.** Twelve drawings, one year of art direction. Solves four grandparent gifts in one upload.

**13. Jewellery.** Several small studios turn a drawing into a pendant or charm bracelet — the drawing, wearable, at grown-up scale.

**14. A keyring.** The five-dollar version of jewellery, and the one most likely to still be on your keys in a decade.

**15. Custom stickers.** A sheet of your child's own characters. They will sticker everything. Accept this before ordering.

**16. Temporary tattoos.** Printable tattoo paper exists, and a child wearing their own dragon on one arm at a birthday party is a core memory in the making.

**17. Phone wallpaper / watch face.** Costs nothing, but earns its place in this tier because you'll see it fifty times a day — the drawing that replaces the stock photo.

## The splurges ($50 and up — birthdays, grandparents, one-per-childhood)

**18. A custom plush.** The famous one. Companies like Budsies have turned hundreds of thousands of drawings into stuffed animals — the wonky legs and crooked smile faithfully reproduced in fabric. Seeing a drawing become a *huggable object* is genuinely uncanny, in the best way.

**19. A hardcover art book.** A year of drawings, professionally printed. We compared [making one yourself vs. using a service](/blog/create-childrens-art-book-at-home/) — both end with a book on the shelf with your child's name on the spine.

**20. Canvas print, gallery-sized.** One drawing, printed a metre wide. The promotion from fridge to gallery wall is the point.

**21. A real tattoo (for you).** The maximalist move: thousands of parents have their child's actual doodle inked, wobbles and all. The wobbles *are* the tattoo. No one regrets the wobbles.

**22. Jewellery in silver.** The heirloom tier — the drawing cast or engraved in actual metal, the kind of object that outlives all of us and gets explained at a wedding forty years from now.

**23. Animated.** Research tools and small studios can rig a drawn character to wave, walk, or dance. For a child, watching their drawing *move* is roughly equivalent to meeting it.

**24. Woven into a blanket.** The drawing, tapestry-sized, on the sofa. Softest possible museum.

**25. A crowd of one.** Commission an artist to paint or illustrate *their* drawing "properly" and frame the two side by side — the original and the tribute. Every artist deserves a cover version.

## Before you order anything: the 60-second rule

Whatever you pick, do two things first, tonight, while the drawing is still on the kitchen table:

**Photograph it.** Companies need a clean image anyway, and paper doesn't wait.

**Ask them about it.** Because here's what we've learned watching thousands of drawings become keepsakes: the plush is wonderful, the mug is wonderful, but in ten years the thing you'll actually ache for is the *explanation* — why the dragon is smiling, who the green person is. The object keeps the drawing safe. Only the story keeps the child in it.

That's the keepsake underneath all 25. Start there, and everything you make from the drawing afterwards gets better.
